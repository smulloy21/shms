Three Levels of Home Modifications


Simple Fixes

Simple fixes include such things as lighting recommendations, shower seats, raised toilet, seats, medication dispensers, magnifiers, lift chairs and ways to adapt your home and lifestyle without making any physical changes to the home itself.

Minor Renovations

The second level of modifications include simple home changes that generally require a handyman to install correctly such as grab bars, hand rails, stair lifts and flooring changes.

Major Remodels

The third level of modifications include the potential home modifications that will change the physical structure of the home such as roll in barrier free shower, making kitchen counters wheelchair accessible, widening doorways to make them walker/wheelchair accessible, and where/how to install an elevator/lift.

How it works

The home safety assessment begins with a personal interview regarding the clients health history, activities they enjoy and want to continue despite potential health restrictions, and daily/nightly routines. Then I complete a thorough home assessment inside and out with notes and photo's to use for the final report.

The final report I complete at home but this is the lengthiest part of the assessment as my final report will include images of the recommendations I make including any adaptive equipment needs and home safety modifications and/or lighting accessories. I send a hard copy in the mail but I can also send a pdf file for faster access to the final home safety report. If the client wishes to have additional input with contractors during the home changes required or assistance in obtaining equipment needed/recommended I can do that too. It's up to them how much involvement they wish to have.

Occupational therapy plays a key role in identifying strategies that enable individuals to modify their homes, thereby maximizing their ability to participate in daily tasks/activities. Occupational therapy practitioners are skilled at recognizing how the environment affects the ability to perform desired occupations. An occupational therapist evaluates balance, coordination, endurance, safety awareness, strength, attention, problem solving, vision, communication, and many other functions while the individual performs daily tasks. In addition to the individual’s performance abilities, occupational therapists also evaluate the home environment to identify barriers to performance. For instance, features can be identified that increase the risk of falls (e.g., loose banisters) or present other hazards (e.g., overloaded electrical outlets). Occupational therapists also review aspects of the home that may require modification to facilitate performance. For example, secure upper-body supports such as handrails or grab bars can assist someone who has difficulty balancing during functional mobility and self-care activities. As part of the evaluation, occupational therapists analyze how a person interacts with his or her environment to complete a task or activity. Through this process, modifications and intervention strategies are selected to improve the fit between these elements, with a goal of maximizing safety and independence in the home. The intervention plan may include but is not limited to strategies such as adaptive equipment, lighting, family caregiver training, or remodeling. - See more at: http://www.aota.org/About-Occupational-Therapy/Professionals/PA/Facts/Home-Modifications.aspx#sthash.3MLzhIR1.dpuf
